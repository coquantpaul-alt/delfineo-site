  US prosecutors have charged Gannon Ken Van Dyke, an active-duty soldier at Fort Bragg, North Carolina, with using classified information about the January raid that captured Venezuelan strongman Nicolás Maduro to place bets on Polymarket that netted more than 400,000 dollars. According to a federal indictment unsealed on Thursday, Van Dyke, who was involved in planning the raid, placed roughly 13 bets worth 33,034 dollars between December and end-January on positions including "US Forces in Venezuela" and "Maduro out". After profiting 409,881 dollars, he moved most of the winnings to a foreign cryptocurrency vault and then to a new online brokerage, and sought to hide his identity following reports of atypical trading on the Maduro contracts.

  Acting US Attorney-General Todd Blanche said soldiers are "prohibited from using this highly sensitive information for personal financial gain", adding that "widespread access to prediction markets is a relatively new phenomenon, but federal laws protecting national security information fully apply". Van Dyke was charged with three counts of violating the Commodity Exchange Act, one count of wire fraud and one count of unlawful monetary transaction — each carrying a maximum sentence of 10 to 20 years. The case was assigned to the Manhattan federal court.

# US soldier involved in Maduro capture charged over $400,000 Polymarket bets using classified information

**The story in one line:** A Fort Bragg soldier allegedly used classified intel about the January Maduro raid to make about 400,000 dollars betting on Polymarket — the first major US insider-trading prosecution tied to a prediction market.

## Key numbers

- **Profit:** **409,881 dollars** over the period December to end-January
- **Bets placed:** **~13 positions**, totalling **33,034 dollars** at stake
- **Counts charged:** 3 violations of Commodity Exchange Act, 1 wire fraud, 1 unlawful monetary transaction — each carrying **10 to 20 years** max
- **Case assigned to:** Manhattan federal court
- **Defendant location:** **Fort Bragg, North Carolina** (active-duty soldier)
- **Funds movement:** most winnings transferred to a foreign cryptocurrency vault, then to a new online brokerage

## Why it matters

The Van Dyke indictment sets a precedent: federal insider-trading laws apply to prediction-market positions when the underlying "asset" is US national-security information. That had been legally ambiguous because prediction markets are commodity derivatives rather than securities. Acting AG Todd Blanche's framing — that "federal laws protecting national security information fully apply" to prediction markets — is the DOJ's clearest public position on that point to date.

Polymarket's response matters too. The platform says it self-flagged the atypical trading around Maduro contracts and referred the matter to the DOJ. If prediction-market operators are going to defend their regulatory standing, self-policing is the minimum bar — this case makes that explicit.

## Takeaway

Two themes converge here: the growing scale of prediction markets as a venue, and the growing regulatory scrutiny. Expect more prosecutions tied to government-insider information, tighter platform identity checks, and likely CFTC or DOJ guidance on the overlap between national-security information and prediction-market trading.
