# Why the UAE asked Pakistan for its $3.5bn back

**The story in one line:** the UAE has called in $3.5bn of central bank deposits from Pakistan, exposing both Islamabad's reserve fragility and a widening Saudi-UAE split inside the Gulf.

## Key numbers

- **$3.5bn**: UAE deposit recalled — about **one-fifth** of Pakistan's central bank reserves.
- **$3bn + $5bn**: Saudi rescue — fresh central bank deposits plus an extended existing line.
- **$7bn**: 2024 IMF bailout the UAE recall threatened to derail; Abu Dhabi had pledged not to seek repayment before the programme ends in 2027.
- **$16bn**: Pakistan's central bank reserves, with Saudi deposits roughly half.
- **$1bn**: planned debt-for-equity swap into the military-linked Fauji Foundation, now cancelled.
- **1.5mn**: Pakistani expatriates in the UAE.

## Why it matters

Three threads converge here. First, Pakistan's reserve buffer is thin enough that one Gulf creditor can swing the macro picture in a week. Second, the UAE's frustration with Islamabad's mediation role and its tilt toward Saudi Arabia is now showing up as financial pressure, not just diplomacy. Third, swapping UAE dependence for Saudi dependence is not free: Riyadh is itself running a wider deficit and tightening liquidity, so the next funding shock has fewer obvious backstops.

## Takeaway

A bilateral loan recall has become a Gulf alignment signal. Watch whether Saudi Arabia translates the defence pact into actual investment — and whether the IMF demands new financing assurances before the next programme review.
