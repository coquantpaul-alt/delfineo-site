# Goldman Sachs leads record renminbi borrowing by US banks as offshore Chinese debt issuance doubles

**The story in one line:** Offshore renminbi issuance ("dim sum" bonds) has more than doubled year-on-year to a record 300 billion yuan, with Goldman Sachs now the biggest foreign issuer — a sign the Chinese currency is picking up the yen's old role as the world's cheap-funding currency.

## Key numbers

- **Dim sum bond issuance YTD:** **300 billion yuan (44 billion dollars)**, more than double the same point last year (itself a record)
- **US banks' self-led issuance:** **47.5 billion yuan**, Goldman majority
- **Goldman's total dim sum issuance YTD:** **32.1 billion yuan** (~10% of total market)
- **Goldman's 10-year dim sum coupon:** **3%** vs **~1.75%** yield on China's 10-year government bond
- **Japan 10-year yield:** **>2.4%** vs **0.61%** at start of 2024 — erasing the yen's cheap-funding edge
- **Other April issuers:** Portugal, Finland's MuniFin, Korea Development Bank, Nordic Investment Bank, Swedish Export Credit Corporation
- **Bond Connect expansion last year:** opened market to Chinese insurers and non-bank financials for the first time

## Why it matters

Two structural shifts converge. On the demand side, Chinese institutional investors need yield to cover liabilities, and Beijing has widened their access to offshore products. On the supply side, foreign issuers — including a US bulge-bracket bank — find that renminbi funding is now cheaper than yen, euro or even dollar. Goldman swaps the proceeds into dollars and deploys the money globally (not in mainland China), so the firm gets a funding arbitrage without mainland capital-controls exposure.

The wider signal is currency internationalisation. Beijing has kept its capital account closed but continues to build the offshore renminbi market as a second-best tool for global renminbi adoption. The pace of foreign borrower adoption this year suggests the tool is working at the margin.

## Takeaway

If the yen-to-renminbi funding-currency shift persists, expect more sovereigns and corporates to tap dim sum in 2026. Watch for European banks and emerging-market sovereigns — they have the widest funding-cost benefit if they are comfortable with the political read-through.
