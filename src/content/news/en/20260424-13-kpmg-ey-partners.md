# KPMG and EY quietly demote equity partners to salaried roles, ending the Big Four's job-for-life model

**The story in one line:** Two of the Big Four are moving equity partners to salaried status — not firing them outright, but stripping the profit share — to concentrate compensation among top performers.

## Key numbers

- **880,000 pounds**: average KPMG UK equity partner pay last year
- **Big Four equity partner promotions**: **five-year low** in 2025
- **2022**: EY introduced its "salaried partner" rung; some equity partners have since been demoted
- **2021**: Jon Holt appointed CEO of KPMG UK; has reallocated partner "units" and outperformed PwC and EY on profit per partner for the first time in over a decade
- **>200 new partnership roles** (salaried + equity) to be created at KPMG UK over two years
- **"Huncs"** (high-units-no-clients): internal KPMG shorthand for partners with big equity stakes and few active clients

## Why it matters

The equity partnership model is the reason working at a Big Four firm has historically been a "golden ticket" — owning a share of the partnership was effectively a retirement annuity. Breaking that — even informally, by relegating partners into salaried roles rather than forcing an exit — signals that the traditional accounting economics do not work any more: consulting demand is slowing, profits are squeezed, and top performers now expect Wall Street-level differentiation.

The salaried-partner device is legally cleaner than firing (no employment contract termination) and optically kinder (the "partner" title stays on the business card). But the incentive effect is the same: a partner without units has no meaningful upside on firm profits.

## Takeaway

Expect the same pattern at Deloitte and PwC — the pressure on Big Four profits is sector-wide. The more important downstream effect is on the talent pipeline: if "partner" no longer reliably means equity, the firms will have to reset the pitch to senior hires and promote candidates.
