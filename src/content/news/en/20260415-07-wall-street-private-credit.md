**The story in one line:** The biggest US banks used strong Q1 earnings to publish, for the first time, the size of their lending exposure to private-credit funds — more than $120bn combined — trying to reassure investors that the stress now visible in Business Development Companies is not a systemic threat.

## Key numbers

- Total disclosed exposure: **>$120 bn** across the big US banks.
- JPMorgan: ~**$50 bn** — Wells Fargo: **$36 bn** — Citi: **$22 bn** — Bank of America: ~**$20 bn**.
- Goldman Sachs raised **$10 bn** for private-credit strategies in Q1.
- Private-credit "at-risk" maturities: concentrated in **2026–2028**, particularly in software / IT loans exposed to AI disruption (Moody's).
- BlackRock AUM: ~**$14 tn** — Fink says institutions are still adding to private credit.

## Why it matters (jargon, explained)

- **Private credit** = direct lending to companies by non-bank funds, typically at higher rates than bank loans. Banks don't make these loans directly any more — instead they lend to the **funds** that do, often via **BDCs** (listed vehicles that package those loans).
- The worry: if those funds take losses on their loans (especially to AI-disrupted software firms), the banks that financed the funds could be next. Dimon last year talked about "cockroaches" in the market; today he says it's "not systemic" but won't rule out "pressures".
- The FSB is preparing a report. The US Treasury has reportedly sent a questionnaire to private-credit managers asking about performance and ties to banks and insurers.

## Takeaway

The US banks are trying to get ahead of regulators by disclosing exposure voluntarily. The narrative is "we can absorb it" — but the timing (FSB report incoming, BDCs selling off, redemptions being gated in retail funds) suggests markets aren't fully convinced. The 2026–2028 refinancing wall, particularly in AI-exposed software debt, is the crystallisation point to watch.
