# China's own Mythos is coming — America must prepare, warns Council on Foreign Relations fellow

**The story in one line:** An ex-White House tech-security official argues the US has roughly seven months before China can match Anthropic's Claude Mythos in AI-enabled cyber warfare, and lays out a maximalist export-control checklist to extend the window.

## Key numbers

- **~7 months**: estimated US lead over China in frontier AI models
- **18 months or more**: lead McGuire thinks is achievable with tightened export controls
- **Claude Mythos Preview** (Anthropic) and **Spud** (OpenAI): models the author says are being withheld from public release and deployed only to select US tech partners for defensive cyber hardening
- **2.5 billion dollars** in Nvidia servers reportedly diverted to China via South-East Asia in one smuggling case
- **2024** ASML DUV lithography machines: China bought more than every other country combined
- **Policy targets for new controls:** all AI chips (including Nvidia H200), smuggling networks, cloud access, remote model access, semiconductor equipment (including foreign-made tools relying on US technology)

## Why it matters

The opinion is a policy position, not news, but its placement in the FT signals that the Mythos release story (see related FT coverage on UK talks with Anthropic for bank access) is entering mainstream political debate. If adopted, the proposed measures would substantially tighten the AI export regime beyond today's settings, including closing the cloud and remote-access loopholes that some Chinese labs exploit. The Cold War analogy — total blockade on offensive capabilities, cooperation on guardrails — would frame the US posture as controlled escalation, not full decoupling.

## Takeaway

Investors should read this as a signal that a tougher chip-control regime is plausible in the coming months. Nvidia's China revenue risk (already impaired) is most exposed; ASML's customer base in China is also in the crosshairs. On the other side, US cyber-security vendors servicing "critical infrastructure hardening" could see budget tailwinds.

*Source: Financial Times, 24 April 2026, Chris McGuire.*
