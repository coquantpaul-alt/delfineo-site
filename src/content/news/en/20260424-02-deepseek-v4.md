# China's DeepSeek launches V4 model, signalling it is not out of the AI race

**The story in one line:** DeepSeek shipped V4 — a lighter Flash and a heavier Pro variant — just as OpenAI ships GPT-5.5 and Washington probes its chip supply chain.

## Key numbers

- **Two variants** released: **V4-Pro** (high-performance, compute-heavy) and **V4-Flash** (cheaper)
- **Not multimodal yet** (no video/image input); DeepSeek says it is working on it
- **100 million yuan (12.5 million euros)**: reported package for Guo Daya, key R1 architect recruited away by ByteDance
- **R1** was released **January 2025** and forced US AI labs into a price recalibration
- **GPT-5.5**: OpenAI's latest model, launched the same week

## Why it matters

DeepSeek built its reputation on cost efficiency — doing at a fraction of the price what US labs did with vast GPU fleets. V4 has to hold that narrative while three things squeeze it. First, US export controls continue to starve Chinese labs of the latest Nvidia GPUs; Washington accuses DeepSeek of sourcing banned Blackwell chips anyway, while The Information claims the model runs on Huawei silicon. Second, domestic rivals Moonshot AI, Zhipu AI and MiniMax have shipped newer versions and are seen as having pulled ahead. Third, Chinese labs have been engaged in a talent auction with very high compensation, draining DeepSeek of key engineers.

The tech industry treats DeepSeek's announcements as a barometer of Chinese AI ambition in a trade-war context. That V4 is still not multimodal — unlike GPT-5.5 and the major Western competitors — is a sign of the cost it has paid for chip access.

## Takeaway

DeepSeek is in the race but not leading it. V4 keeps the lights on, but the real test is the multimodal version and whether Huawei-optimised inference can actually match Nvidia-optimised stacks at scale. The Huawei angle is the bigger signal than the model itself.
