# Private equity backers raise new conflict concerns over continuation-vehicle deals

**The story in one line:** big LPs are calling out the conflict of interest when their peers vote to approve buyout firms selling assets to themselves — and the firms voting also back the buyer.

## Key numbers

- **~1/5**: share of all PE exits done via continuation vehicles last year.
- **>$100bn**: 2024 continuation-vehicle sales globally (Jefferies), up from about **$70bn** in 2023.
- **≤$7bn**: comparable 2015 figure — a market that did not really exist a decade ago.
- **Hamilton Lane, HarbourVest, Partners Group**: named multi-strategy firms that run both buyout fund investing and secondaries businesses.

## Why it matters

Continuation vehicles let a manager keep its winners and avoid marking down its losers in a bad exit market. That is useful, but it depends on a credible price discovery process between the seller (the old fund) and the buyer (the continuation vehicle). When the institutions sitting on the seller's advisory committee also have business lines investing in the buyer, the price discovery process is no longer independent. One executive described seeing multi-strategy firms use future buyout-fund commitments as a bargaining chip to win cheaper access to the same firm's continuation vehicle — exactly the kind of side payment ILPA is now asking to be disclosed.

## Takeaway

This is the next governance front in private capital. Expect more LPs to demand wider committee votes, full disclosure of side incentives, and recusal when a firm sits on both sides. For underlying investors — including pension beneficiaries — it matters because the price at which the asset rolls into the new vehicle defines their return on the way out.
