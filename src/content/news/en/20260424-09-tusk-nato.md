# Poland's Tusk questions whether the US is 'loyal' to NATO and urges EU to become a 'real alliance'

**The story in one line:** Poland — NATO's biggest per-GDP spender — says openly that an Article 5 response from the US cannot be taken for granted and pushes the EU to activate its own Article 42.7 mutual-defence clause.

## Key numbers

- **5% of GDP**: Poland's defence spending, equal to NATO's target and above the alliance average
- **~20 Russian drones**: 2025 breach of Polish airspace; some NATO allies hesitated to treat it as an attack
- **Article 5** (NATO) vs **Article 42.7** (EU): the two mutual-defence clauses under discussion
- **"months, not years"**: Tusk's timing estimate for a potential Russian attack on an alliance member
- **2022**: last time NATO forces directly confronted Russian assets before last year's drone incident
- **Péter Magyar** (pro-EU conservative) seen as likely next Hungarian PM, replacing **Viktor Orbán** (Putin ally)

## Why it matters

A Polish prime minister questioning US loyalty to NATO — from the most Atlanticist EU member state — is a political signal beyond the words themselves. It formalises the continental debate around Article 42.7, previously a dormant provision, and creates political cover for Germany, France and the Commission to move on joint defence infrastructure (anti-drone capabilities, joint procurement, military mobility).

Ursula von der Leyen said this spring that the EU should bring Article 42.7 "to life". But many member states worry that overt EU defence co-ordination will be read as undermining NATO and give Trump reason to disengage further. Orbán's imminent departure removes one veto; Tusk's intervention gives the summit political momentum.

## Takeaway

This is less a policy announcement than a permission slip. Tusk is telling other European capitals that it is now acceptable to say publicly what most have been saying privately about US reliability. Watch the Cyprus summit's final communiqué for concrete Article 42.7 activation language.
