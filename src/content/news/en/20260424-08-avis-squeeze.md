# Avis Budget surges 342% in weeks as a hedge-fund stand-off threatens a short squeeze of GameStop proportions

**The story in one line:** Avis Budget is up 342% in five weeks because Pentwater and SRS together control a position bigger than the free float and short sellers cannot find shares to buy back.

## Key numbers

- **+342%** since 20 March to **444 dollars**; S&P 500 up **10%** over the same period
- **SRS Investment Management:** **~17 million shares (~50%)** of the 35 million outstanding
- **Pentwater Capital Management:** **8 million shares (22%)**, disclosed on **20 March**
- **Additional swap rights:** up to **13 million more shares** between the two funds
- **Free float outside the two:** **10 million shares**
- **Short interest:** **9 million shares** sold short
- **Paper losses for short sellers:** **2.5 billion dollars** (S3 Partners)
- **Share borrow cost:** **>13x** normal levels
- **New share authorisation:** up to **5 million** shares (needs board approval; SRS holds 2 of 6 seats)

## Why it matters

Avis Budget's squeeze is the cleanest example in years of a position larger than the tradable float. Both SRS and Pentwater are legally constrained from selling (SRS by quiet-period rules, Pentwater by the short-swing rule until September). Dealers who sold the swaps hedge by holding the stock themselves. Short sellers have nowhere to go.

The precedent most investors cite is GameStop 2021 (retail-driven) and Volkswagen 2008 (Porsche's derivative position). The Avis story combines both dynamics: a concentrated institutional squeeze with retail piling on.

## Takeaway

The trade is now a structural squeeze, not a fundamental call on Avis. The short side is bleeding 2.5 billion dollars of paper losses, and the unwind path is narrow. Watch the late-April earnings and any follow-on share issuance — those are the only realistic pressure valves.
